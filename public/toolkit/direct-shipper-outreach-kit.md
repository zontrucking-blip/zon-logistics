# Direct Shipper Outreach Kit

## How to use this

Everything in here assumes you have zero sales training. You don't need it. You need to sound like you know what you're doing, give the shipper one reason to try you, and stop talking before you talk yourself out of it.

Read the scripts out loud before you use them. They'll feel stiff the first few times. That's fine. The structure matters more than the exact words, so make it yours once you know the shape of it.

---

## 1. Opening Cold Call Script

The goal on call one isn't to close a deal. It's to get them curious enough to take your email or agree to a callback. Anything beyond that is a bonus.

**Have these ready before you dial:**

- Their name (first name if you have it)
- Your lane, specific: "Chicago to Cincinnati" not "the Midwest"
- Your carrier packet, ready to send the moment they ask

---

**The call:**

*[They pick up]*

"Hey, is this [Name]? Good. My name is [Your Name], I run [Company]. We're a carrier, not a broker, and we run [your lane or region]. Got 60 seconds?"

*[If yes:]*

"Who handles your freight [in/out of your area] right now? And when you book through them, are you dealing with a broker or going straight to the truck?"

*[Listen. Then:]*

"Reason I'm asking: I'm building direct relationships with shippers who are tired of paying broker margins and not knowing who actually has their load. We own our equipment. I'm the one who picks up when you call. On the lanes we run, I can usually match or beat what you're paying a broker, and you'd be talking to the truck, not the middleman."

"Is there a lane you run regularly where it'd make sense to try a direct carrier for one load?"

*[If they're open:]*

"Perfect. What's the best email? I'll send our carrier packet tonight."

*[If they're not ready:]*

"No problem. Can I follow up in a few weeks? Freight markets shift and it might make more sense then."

---

**A note on delivery:** after a few calls, you'll stop reading it and just know the shape. Who you are, what you do, one question to get them talking, one reason to try you, one ask. Stay in that structure and you'll be fine.

If they answer the "broker or direct?" question by naming a broker, you already have your opening.

## 2. Objection Handlers

Shippers say the same five things. You'll hear these on most calls. The answers below aren't designed to trick anyone; they're designed to give the shipper a real reason to keep talking if one exists.

Practice them out loud before you need them.

---

**"We already use a broker."**

"That makes sense, most shippers do. Can I ask: do you know what that broker is making on your loads? Carriers typically see about 75 to 80 cents of every dollar you pay. The rest goes to the broker for making phone calls. If you worked with us directly on one or two lanes, that money either stays in your pocket or goes to the truck that's actually moving your freight. Worth trying on one load?"

---

**"We don't work with small carriers."**

"I hear that. Usually it comes down to insurance and reliability. Is that what you mean, or is there something specific?"

*[Let them answer.]*

"We carry $1 million in general liability and $100,000 in cargo coverage, same as the nationals. The real difference is when something goes sideways, you're not calling a broker who calls a dispatcher who calls a driver. You call me. That either matters to you or it doesn't, but I'd rather you base it on that than on headcount."

---

**"Send me something in writing."**

"Done. What's the best email? I'll have our carrier packet to you in 20 minutes."

*[Stop talking. Get the email.]*

"Got it. Thursday or Friday, which is better to follow up?"

This objection is genuine about half the time. If they give you the email without hesitation, they're interested. If they drag their feet, treat it as a soft no and follow up once.

---

**"Not interested."**

"Fair enough. Can I ask what's working well right now? I'm not trying to fix something that isn't broken."

*[If they say a broker is handling it fine:]*

"Understood. If anything changes with rates or service and you want to try a direct carrier, I'm easy to reach. Can I check back in a couple of months?"

Don't push past two attempts. If they say no again, thank them and hang up clean. Pushing a third time costs you the future call.

---

**"Call me back in 6 months."**

"I'll do that. What's the best day and time so I'm not catching you at a bad moment?"

*[Lock in a specific date, not a month.]*

"One quick thing before I let you go: is there a lane or season where things get tight for you? That way when I call back I'll come with something useful instead of just a check-in."

Put it in your calendar the second you hang up. Call back on the exact date. Most carriers forget and that alone makes you stand out.

## 3. Follow-Up Email Sequence

Three emails. That's the sequence. Not five, not a weekly newsletter, not an automated drip that sounds like it came from a software company. Three emails at the right spacing and then you stop unless they give you a reason to continue.

---

**Email 1: Same day or next morning after the call**

> **Subject:** Carrier packet — [Your Company Name]
>
> [First name],
>
> Good talking with you [this morning / earlier]. Attached is our carrier packet: authority, insurance certificates, and the lanes we run.
>
> We move [freight type] on [your lane or region]. Rates are competitive with what brokers charge and you'd be working directly with us, no middleman.
>
> If a load comes up where it makes sense to give us a shot, call or text me directly: [your number].
>
> [Your name]
> [Company] | MC# [number] | [phone]

---

**Email 2: Four days after the first call**

> **Subject:** Re: Carrier packet — [Your Company Name]
>
> [First name],
>
> Following up on what I sent [Monday / the other day]. If you've got a [your lane] load coming up in the next few weeks, I'd like to quote it.
>
> No commitment. Send me the details and I'll get you a number the same day.
>
> [Your name] | [phone]

---

**Email 3: Ten days after the first call**

> **Subject:** Last note from [Your Name] at [Company]
>
> [First name],
>
> Last one from me for a while. If the timing isn't right, no hard feelings.
>
> If you ever want to try a direct carrier on a lane where your broker has been expensive or inconsistent, we're here. I keep a short list of shippers I want to work with and you're on it.
>
> [Your name] | [phone]

## 4. Voicemail Script

Most voicemails don't get returned because they waste the first ten seconds explaining who the caller is and then say the number too fast at the end. This script fixes both problems.

**The script:**

"[Name], this is [Your Name] at [Company]. We're a carrier running [your lane, e.g. Chicago to the Northeast], and I'm calling because I want to move some of your freight directly and cut the broker out of the middle. I'll keep it short when you call back. Number is [your number]. I'll say it twice: [your number]. Thanks."

**Why it works:** you gave them a reason before they decided not to call back. Saying "carrier running Chicago to the Northeast" tells them in four words whether you're relevant to them. "Cut the broker out" is something no broker can say. Two slow repetitions of the number means they don't have to replay the voicemail.

Keep it under 25 seconds. If you're running long, cut something.

---

## 5. One-Liner

For when someone asks what you do, or why they should use you instead of their broker:

**"We own the truck. When you call, the person who answers is the person driving it."**

Sixteen words. Use it.
